You May Not Need as Much Saved as You Think

A Colorado Homebuyer Guide for First Responders, Teachers, Nurses, Firefighters, and Public Service Workers

Last reviewed June 2026

Most Colorado public service workers assume homeownership means years of saving before the numbers work. Depending on your situation, the timeline may be shorter than you think, and the upfront cash you need could be lower than you've been told.

Colorado has several homebuyer assistance programs, including state-backed options and programs created specifically for first responders, educators, and public service professionals. None of them are simple, none of them are automatic, and none of them are for everyone. But if you have not explored whether one of these paths fits your situation, you may be waiting longer than you need to.

This guide explains what is available, what the real numbers look like, and what questions to ask before you choose a lender or a loan.

The Short Version

Colorado has several homebuyer assistance programs that may reduce the upfront cash needed to buy a home, depending on your situation. Programs worth knowing include CHFA (Colorado's statewide housing finance resource), MetroDPA (a front range and statewide option), SB26-053 (a 2026 bill designed to expand CHFA access for first responders), SB25-167 (which created a shared equity homeownership program for Colorado public school employees, signed into law in 2025), and VA loans for eligible veterans and active-duty service members. Eligibility, lender participation, and program rules matter in every case. These are paths to explore, not guarantees.

Why Colorado Public Service Workers Have Unique Options

Colorado has invested in housing programs that go beyond generic first-time buyer assistance. Some programs are designed specifically for public service professionals. Others are broad programs that public service workers may access alongside the rest of the buyer pool.

The distinction matters. Programs like SB26-053, which was introduced in the 2026 session and is designed to expand CHFA mortgage eligibility to law enforcement officers and first responders, and SB25-167, which became law in June 2025 and created the Educator First Home Ownership Program for public school employees, are rooted in Colorado legislation. They are not private referral programs or national networks that pay commissions for buyer introductions. They are state-backed efforts to make homeownership more accessible for people who serve Colorado communities.

CHFA: Colorado's Statewide Homebuyer Resource

CHFA, the Colorado Housing and Finance Authority, is the state's primary housing finance resource for homebuyers. CHFA does not lend money directly. It works through a statewide network of approved lenders who originate CHFA-backed loans and assistance options.

According to CHFA's official site, homebuyers using a CHFA first mortgage may be eligible for one of two assistance options:

  • CHFA DPA Grant: Up to the lesser of $25,000 or 3% of the first mortgage amount. No repayment required. A higher interest rate on the first mortgage applies.
  • CHFA Second Mortgage Loan: Up to the lesser of $25,000 or 4% of the first mortgage amount. Repayment is deferred until you sell, refinance, pay off the first mortgage, or the home is no longer your primary residence. A higher interest rate applies.

There are also specific CHFA programs for first-generation homebuyers that may offer up to $25,000 regardless of the first mortgage amount, depending on program rules.

Planning example (illustrative only): On a $500,000 home with a $475,000 first mortgage, a CHFA DPA grant could represent up to approximately $14,250 (3% of $475,000), and a CHFA second mortgage could represent up to approximately $19,000 (4% of $475,000), both depending on program rules, eligibility, and lender participation. These are planning ranges. Actual amounts depend on the specific loan and program path.

CHFA requires homebuyer education and connects buyers with CHFA participating lenders across Colorado.

SB26-053: The Bill Designed to Expand CHFA Access for First Responders

SB26-053 is best understood as a legislative effort to expand the pool of Colorado buyers eligible for CHFA mortgage loans. Introduced in the 2026 session, the bill was designed to make law enforcement officers and first responders eligible for CHFA mortgages, with income limits applying to families made newly eligible by the bill.

This is not a separate standalone program. It is an expansion of CHFA eligibility, meaning first responders and law enforcement officers who become eligible under this bill would access CHFA's existing loan and assistance structure, including the down payment assistance options described above.

Important framing note: As of this writing, SB26-053 was working through the Colorado legislative process. Final terms still depend on how the bill was enacted and how CHFA implements any resulting changes. If you are a first responder or law enforcement officer exploring CHFA, speaking with a CHFA participating lender directly is the right next step for current eligibility details.

Who this may benefit: Firefighters, police officers, sheriff's deputies, EMTs, paramedics, 911 operators, and other first responders in Colorado who have not previously qualified for CHFA programs due to income restrictions.

SB25-167: The Colorado Educator First Home Ownership Program

SB25-167 became law on June 4, 2025, when Governor Polis signed it. The law creates what is formally called the Educator First Home Ownership Program within Colorado's public school fund. The program is designed to expand access to homeownership for public school employees through a shared equity down payment assistance structure.

What shared equity means in plain English: Shared equity programs provide upfront assistance in exchange for a share of the home's appreciation when the property is sold or refinanced. Rather than a lump-sum gift, the program participates in the home's future value. This structure is designed to help the program serve more buyers over time.

Under SB25-167, the state treasurer is directed to invest in this program using interest earned on the public school fund. The shared equity DPA program is required to be established by July 1, 2026 and prioritizes first-time homebuyers who use the home as their primary residence.

Who this may benefit: Colorado public school employees, including teachers and other school staff, who are first-time homebuyers.

Important framing note: SB25-167 creates a new program that is still being established. Final eligibility criteria, down payment amounts, and program manager details depend on implementation decisions that are being made now. Use language like "worth exploring" and "final terms still depend on program implementation" when discussing this program with a lender.

Source: Colorado General Assembly, SB25-167

MetroDPA: A Statewide Option Worth Knowing About

MetroDPA is a down payment assistance program administered through the City and County of Denver but available to buyers throughout Colorado. It offers a 30-year, fixed-rate first mortgage paired with a zero percent second mortgage that provides assistance for down payment, closing costs, or prepaids.

Key facts from MetroDPA's official program materials:

  • Assistance is in the form of a 30-year second mortgage at 0% interest with no scheduled monthly payments
  • No first-time homebuyer requirement, available to repeat buyers as well
  • Minimum FICO credit score: as low as 620 (640 in some cases)
  • Income limit: qualifying income below $210,150
  • No purchase price limits within the program
  • Available loan types: FHA, VA, USDA-RD, and conventional
  • Homebuyer education is required

Planning example (illustrative only): On a $500,000 home with a note amount of $500,000, assistance at a 6% rate would represent up to $30,000 in planning terms. The actual assistance percentage and amount depend on the specific program path, lender, and eligibility at the time of application. This is a planning illustration, not a guaranteed amount.

For current program details, contact a MetroDPA participating lender.

VA Loans for Colorado Veterans and Active-Duty Service Members

A VA loan is one of the most powerful homebuying tools available to eligible veterans, active-duty service members, and qualifying military spouses. It may allow you to purchase a home with no down payment. But zero down is not the same as zero cash at closing, and this distinction matters when you are planning.

What VA loan eligibility generally does not cover:

  • Closing costs (lender fees, title fees, escrow deposits)
  • Prepaid expenses (homeowners insurance, property taxes)
  • Home inspection costs
  • Earnest money deposit
  • VA funding fee (unless exempt due to service-connected disability)

Depending on the transaction, seller credits negotiated at the time of purchase may help reduce some of these costs. Certain assistance programs may also apply alongside a VA loan depending on eligibility and program rules. This varies by situation, and not all DPA programs are available with VA financing.

For Colorado veterans exploring their options, the key is understanding the full picture of upfront costs before choosing a loan path. Learn more on our programs page.

Three Colorado Buyer Scenarios: Planning Examples

These are illustrative examples only. None of these scenarios represent a guarantee, an eligibility determination, or a specific dollar amount you will receive. Program rules, amounts, and eligibility vary. Use these as a starting framework, not a projection.

Scenario 1: A Colorado First Responder Comparing Options

A firefighter in the Denver metro area is exploring a $500,000 home purchase. She earns $85,000 annually and has a FICO score of 680. She has not owned a home before.

CHFA path (planning example): With a $475,000 first mortgage, a CHFA DPA grant could represent up to approximately $14,250 (3% of $475,000) depending on program rules, income limits, and lender. A CHFA second mortgage could represent up to approximately $19,000 (4%), deferred until sale or refinance. These paths carry a higher interest rate on the first mortgage.

SB26-053 consideration: If SB26-053 expanded CHFA eligibility for first responders as designed, she may access CHFA's programs even if prior income restrictions had excluded her. Final terms depend on enactment and program implementation. Verifying with a CHFA lender is the right step.

MetroDPA path (planning example): If she meets income limits (below $210,150) and uses a MetroDPA participating lender, she may be eligible for a zero percent second mortgage for a percentage of the loan amount. At a 6% planning example on a $500,000 note, this could represent up to $30,000 in assistance, depending on current program terms.

Seller credits: In a negotiated transaction, the seller might contribute toward closing costs, which could reduce the cash needed at the table. This depends on the property, the market, the loan type, and the negotiation.

Important note: CHFA and MetroDPA are separate programs. Whether they can be combined depends on program rules and lender participation. Do not assume they stack automatically.

Scenario 2: A Colorado Teacher Exploring SB25-167 and CHFA

A fourth-grade teacher at a Denver public school is a first-time buyer looking at a $450,000 home. She earns $62,000 and has a FICO score of 710.

SB25-167 consideration: As a public school employee and first-time buyer, she may be among the intended beneficiaries of the Educator First Home Ownership Program created under SB25-167. The program is designed to provide shared equity down payment assistance, meaning she may receive assistance now in exchange for sharing a portion of the home's appreciation when she eventually sells or refinances. Final program details depend on implementation, which is underway with a July 1, 2026 target launch.

CHFA path (planning example): With a $427,500 first mortgage (5% down), a CHFA grant could represent up to approximately $12,825 (3% of $427,500) or a deferred second mortgage up to approximately $17,100 (4%), depending on income limits and program rules.

Key question: Whether SB25-167 assistance and CHFA assistance can be used together depends on how the Educator First Home Ownership Program is structured and what lenders participate. Getting current information from a lender who knows both programs is essential.

Scenario 3: A Colorado Veteran Planning for Closing Costs

A Colorado Army veteran with full VA home loan entitlement is buying a $525,000 home. He has a service-connected disability rating that waives the VA funding fee.

VA loan path: He may be able to purchase with no down payment. Because he is exempt from the VA funding fee due to his disability rating, one of the typical upfront costs is eliminated.

What remains: He still needs to plan for closing costs, which may include lender fees, title insurance, homeowners insurance escrow, property tax escrow, and the home inspection. On a $525,000 purchase, these could range from $8,000 to $15,000 or more depending on the lender, location, and transaction specifics.

Seller credits: In the current Colorado market, negotiating seller credits toward closing costs is possible, though it depends on the property, competition, and seller. On a VA loan, seller concessions up to 4% of the purchase price are permitted.

DPA with VA loans: Some assistance programs are compatible with VA financing. Whether a specific program applies depends on lender participation and program rules at the time of application. A lender experienced with VA loans and Colorado DPA programs is the right resource.

A Note on Private "Hero Home Buying" Offers

If you have searched for homebuying help for first responders or teachers, you have probably come across national programs that market directly to public service workers. Some of these programs offer rebates or referral benefits rather than state-backed assistance.

Private programs operated by real estate companies, lender networks, or national organizations are not the same as state-funded assistance programs like CHFA, MetroDPA, or the programs created by SB25-167 and SB26-053. The structure, benefits, and who profits are different. Understanding the difference matters before you commit to a lender or buyer's agent relationship.

How to Compare Your Colorado Homebuying Options

The programs described in this guide are separate paths. Eligibility, assistance amounts, interest rates, and repayment structures vary across each one. Before you choose a lender, it is worth understanding which programs may apply to your situation and what trade-offs each path involves.

Key questions to bring to any lender conversation:

  • Which CHFA loan and DPA options apply to my income and purchase price?
  • Am I eligible for MetroDPA and which assistance percentage is currently available?
  • As a first responder or educator, do I have access to any programs through SB26-053 or SB25-167?
  • If I am a veteran, what closing costs should I plan for after my zero-down VA loan?
  • Can any programs be combined for my loan type and situation?
  • What is the total cost difference between the grant path and the second mortgage path over time?

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Colorado first responders combine multiple assistance programs?

Whether multiple programs can be used together depends on each program's rules, the loan type, and lender participation. Combining programs is not automatic. A qualified lender familiar with CHFA and other Colorado programs can help you understand what combinations may be possible for your situation.

Is CHFA only for first-time home buyers in Colorado?

Not always. While CHFA programs generally include income and purchase price limits, eligibility requirements vary by program path. Some CHFA options are available to repeat buyers. Speaking with a CHFA participating lender is the best way to understand current program rules for your situation.

Does a VA loan mean I pay nothing at closing?

No. A VA loan may allow eligible veterans and active-duty service members to purchase a home with no down payment, but closing costs, escrows, inspections, earnest money, and potentially a VA funding fee may still apply. Seller credits and certain assistance programs could help reduce upfront cash depending on the situation, loan type, and program rules.

What is the difference between a DPA grant and a forgivable loan in Colorado?

A grant generally does not require repayment. A forgivable loan may be forgiven after a certain period or under certain conditions. A deferred second mortgage requires repayment but postpones it until events like sale, refinance, or payoff of the first mortgage. Program structures vary, so reviewing the terms of any specific program is important before you commit.
