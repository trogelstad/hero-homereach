Can Nurses Buy a Home With Student Loans in Colorado?

Buyer Education Healthcare Workers June 2026 8 min read
Last reviewed: June 2026
Share

Buying a home as a nurse can feel frustrating. You spent years investing in your career. You worked long shifts, earned your degree and took on student debt to get there.

Now you may be wondering if those loans have quietly closed the door on homeownership.

The good news is simple: student loans do not automatically prevent you from buying a home in Colorado. Many nurses and healthcare workers buy homes while carrying student debt. The key is understanding how lenders actually review those loans.

Quick Answer

Yes. Nurses can buy a home with student loans in Colorado.

Student debt by itself does not disqualify you. Lenders focus on your monthly student loan payment, debt to income ratio, credit profile, income, loan type and available assistance options. Programs like CHFA, MetroDPA and Chenoa Fund may also help reduce the cash needed at closing, depending on eligibility.

Why Student Loans Feel So Scary for Nurses

Healthcare workers often carry serious education debt. Registered nurses, nurse practitioners, physical therapists, occupational therapists, respiratory therapists, radiology technicians and many other medical professionals may graduate with tens of thousands of dollars in loans.

That creates a common fear:

If I still have student loans, I probably cannot buy a home yet.

Many people delay their plans for years because of that assumption. But mortgage approval is rarely about whether you have debt. It is about how that debt fits into your overall financial picture.

The Number Lenders Really Care About

Lenders pay close attention to your debt to income ratio, often called DTI. Think of DTI as a snapshot of your monthly obligations compared with your monthly income.

Your monthly debt picture may include:

  • Future mortgage payment
  • Student loans
  • Car loans
  • Credit cards
  • Personal loans
  • Other recurring debts

The lower your monthly obligations compared with your income, the stronger your application may be. This means two nurses with identical student loan balances could have very different mortgage outcomes.

One may qualify comfortably. Another may need to adjust the loan type, repayment plan, purchase price or timing.

Plain English takeaway: Your total student loan balance matters less than the monthly payment a lender must count. That is the number that can shift your approval math.

How Student Loans Are Counted by Loan Type

Different mortgage programs can treat student loans differently. That is why one lender answer should not automatically end the conversation.

FHA Loans

FHA loans remain popular with first time buyers because they can allow lower down payments and more flexible credit profiles. FHA generally requires lenders to count student loans as part of the monthly debt review. If a payment is reported and documented, that payment may be used. If the payment is zero or not reported, FHA may require a calculated payment based on the outstanding balance.

Conventional Loans

Conventional financing follows different guidelines. In some situations, a documented income driven repayment amount may be used. That can matter for nurses who have a lower payment under an income based plan.

VA Loans

Veterans, active duty military members and some military connected healthcare workers may have access to VA financing through the VA home loan program. VA loans can allow zero down payment for eligible buyers, but closing costs and debt review still matter.

USDA Loans

Certain rural areas of Colorado may qualify for USDA financing. USDA loans have their own income, property and student loan rules. They can be useful in the right location, but they are not available everywhere.

A $500,000 Colorado Example

Let us imagine two Colorado nurses looking at a $500,000 home.

Buyer Student Loan Balance Monthly Student Loan Payment Why It Matters
Nurse A $80,000 $250 documented payment The lower monthly payment may create more room in the debt to income calculation.
Nurse B $80,000 Higher calculated payment The same balance may create a tougher qualifying picture if the lender must count a higher monthly payment.

Both nurses owe the same amount. Their mortgage math may still look very different.

That is why it is dangerous to assume your student loan balance alone decides whether you can buy. The details matter.

Where CHFA, MetroDPA and Chenoa Fit

Student loans affect debt to income ratios. They do not automatically create down payment money. That is where Colorado homebuyer assistance programs may become valuable.

CHFA

The Colorado Housing and Finance Authority offers several homeownership programs through participating lenders. CHFA may provide down payment assistance as a grant or a deferred second mortgage, depending on the program selected.

CHFA is not only for nurses and it is not a direct lender. Healthcare workers may use CHFA when they meet the program requirements. For a deeper breakdown, read our guide to CHFA explained in plain English.

MetroDPA

MetroDPA may be available in many Front Range communities and can be used by first time or repeat buyers when program rules are met. The assistance is tied to the first mortgage amount and can help with upfront costs.

On a $500,000 mortgage example, six percent assistance would equal approximately $30,000. That number sounds powerful, but the repayment structure and lender rules still matter. Review the details in our MetroDPA Colorado guide.

Chenoa Fund

The Chenoa Fund is another possible assistance path for eligible borrowers. Depending on the option used, assistance may be forgivable or repayable. Nurses should review the structure carefully before assuming it works like a grant.

Important: Most major Colorado assistance programs are profession neutral. There may not be a special nurse grant, but nurses can still explore the same public assistance paths available to other qualified buyers.

What Nurses Should Gather Before Talking With a Lender

Getting organized early can make the process smoother. Nurses often have income that is more complex than a simple salary. Overtime, shift differential, weekend pay, PRN work and per diem income may all need documentation.

Helpful documents include:

  • Recent pay stubs
  • W-2 forms
  • Student loan statements
  • Income driven repayment documentation
  • Tax returns if required
  • Bank statements
  • Overtime history
  • Shift differential records
  • PRN or per diem income history if applicable

Many nurses earn more than their base pay suggests. When properly documented, that income may strengthen the mortgage conversation.

Common Mistakes Healthcare Workers Make

Waiting Until Every Student Loan Is Paid Off

For many buyers, waiting until every student loan is gone could delay homeownership for years. Paying down debt may help in some situations, but it is not always required before buying.

Assuming Student Loan Forgiveness Means Loans Are Ignored

Programs like Public Service Loan Forgiveness can be valuable, but they do not automatically remove student loans from mortgage calculations. Lenders still need to review the payment and documentation.

Believing One Lender's Answer Is Final

Different loan types and lenders may interpret the file differently. If one answer does not make sense, it may be worth asking better questions before giving up.

Assuming Down Payment Assistance Solves Debt to Income Problems

Assistance may help reduce the cash needed at closing. It does not automatically improve debt to income ratios. That means the student loan payment still matters.

There Is No Special Nurse Grant in Colorado

This surprises many healthcare workers. Colorado does not currently offer a dedicated statewide nurse grant.

Instead, nurses often use broader homebuyer assistance options such as:

  • CHFA
  • MetroDPA
  • Chenoa Fund
  • VA loans if eligible
  • USDA loans in eligible rural areas
  • Local city or county assistance programs
  • Seller credits where allowed

The challenge is not usually finding a secret nurse program. The challenge is understanding which path fits your income, student loan payment, savings, credit, location and timeline.

That is where education becomes valuable. Start with our Colorado homebuyer assistance programs page or review common homebuyer assistance questions before your first lender conversation.

Best Next Steps for Colorado Nurses

  1. 1

    Find Your Actual Student Loan Payment

    Do not guess. Pull your current student loan statement and repayment plan documentation. The monthly payment is usually more important than the balance.

  2. 2

    Review Your Income Pattern

    Gather pay stubs and look at base pay, overtime, shift differential, weekend pay and PRN income. A lender may need history before counting variable income.

  3. 3

    Compare Loan Types

    FHA, conventional, VA and USDA loans may treat student loans differently. The right path depends on your full profile.

  4. 4

    Explore Colorado Assistance Options

    CHFA, MetroDPA, Chenoa Fund and local programs may help reduce upfront cash needs. Final terms still depend on program rules and lender review.

  5. 5

    Ask the Right Questions Before Applying

    Ask how your student loan payment will be counted, whether your income pattern can be used and which assistance programs may fit your situation.

Bottom Line

A nursing career often requires years of sacrifice. Student loans are part of that journey for many healthcare professionals.

But having student loans does not automatically put homeownership out of reach.

Sometimes the biggest obstacle is not student debt. It is simply not knowing what to ask next.
