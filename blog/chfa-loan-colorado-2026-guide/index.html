CHFA Loan Colorado 2026: Down Payment Help for Hero Homebuyers

Program Guide All Heroes Last reviewed May 2026 16 min read
Share

Buying a home in Colorado can feel possible on paper until the upfront cash number shows up.

You may have the income. You may have the job stability. You may even be ready for the monthly payment.

But then you see the down payment, closing costs, prepaid taxes, insurance, appraisal, inspection, and cash-to-close estimate.

That is where many Colorado buyers get stuck.

For teachers, first responders, healthcare workers, veterans, active-duty military members, and military spouses, that can feel especially frustrating. You serve Colorado communities every day, yet buying in those same communities can feel just out of reach.

That is why CHFA loans in Colorado are worth understanding. CHFA is not only for heroes. But for many public service buyers, CHFA may be one of the first places to look when trying to reduce the upfront cash needed to buy a home.

Quick Answer

A CHFA loan can help eligible Colorado buyers access fixed-rate mortgage options plus down payment and closing cost assistance.

CHFA's grant option may provide up to the lesser of $25,000 or 3% of the first mortgage, with no repayment required.

CHFA's second mortgage option may provide up to the lesser of $25,000 or 4% of the first mortgage, with repayment deferred until certain events such as sale, refinance, payoff of the first mortgage, or the home no longer being your primary residence.

What Is a CHFA Loan in Colorado?

CHFA stands for the Colorado Housing and Finance Authority.

It is a statewide Colorado housing resource that helps expand access to affordable and responsible homeownership. Through a network of approved lenders, CHFA offers home loan programs that may be paired with down payment and closing cost assistance.

Here is the key thing to understand: you do not apply directly to CHFA like you would apply directly to a bank.

Instead, you work with a CHFA Participating Lender. That lender helps you review which CHFA loan program, assistance option, and mortgage path may fit your situation.

That matters because CHFA is not just one simple loan. It is more like a menu of Colorado homebuyer tools. Depending on your situation, a CHFA path may include:

  • A 30-year fixed-rate mortgage
  • Down payment assistance
  • Closing cost assistance
  • A grant option
  • A deferred second mortgage option
  • Program-specific paths for certain buyers
  • Required homebuyer education

For a broader look at assistance paths available in Colorado, visit Hero HomeReach's guide to Colorado homebuyer assistance programs.

Why CHFA Matters for Colorado Hero Homebuyers

Colorado home prices have made the upfront cash problem harder.

Many public service professionals are not wondering whether they want to buy. They are wondering how to get over the cash-to-close wall without draining every dollar they have saved.

A nurse in Castle Rock. A teacher in Colorado Springs. A firefighter in Aurora. A veteran buying near Denver. A sheriff's deputy trying to stay close to the community they serve.

Different jobs. Different loan paths. Same basic question: How much cash do I really need to get into a home?

CHFA may help answer part of that question.

It does not guarantee approval. It does not erase every cost. It does not mean every buyer qualifies. But it may give eligible buyers a way to reduce the amount of money they need upfront.

That can change the conversation.

Instead of thinking, "I need to save another $20,000 before I can even start," you may be able to ask, "Which assistance path could fit my numbers?" That is a much better question to be asking.

If you are a first responder, you may also want to read about the Colorado Champions Home Loan Program, which recently expanded CHFA mortgage access for firefighters, EMTs, and other public safety professionals.

CHFA Down Payment Assistance: Grant vs. Second Mortgage

The biggest CHFA decision most buyers need to understand is the difference between the grant option and the second mortgage option.

Both may help with your down payment and/or closing costs. But they are not the same thing.

CHFA Grant Option

The CHFA Down Payment Assistance Grant may provide up to the lesser of $25,000 or 3% of your first mortgage.

The biggest benefit: CHFA describes this grant option as assistance with no repayment required.

That can be powerful if your main goal is to reduce upfront cash without adding a second loan that has to be repaid later. For buyers who want the cleanest structure, the grant path may be the more attractive option.

The tradeoff is that the grant amount is generally smaller than the second mortgage option.

CHFA Second Mortgage Option

The CHFA second mortgage option may provide up to the lesser of $25,000 or 4% of your first mortgage.

This may provide more assistance than the grant path. But it is not a grant.

It is a second mortgage with repayment deferred until certain events happen. That may include:

  • You sell the home
  • You refinance the first mortgage
  • You pay off the first mortgage
  • The home is no longer your primary residence

The second mortgage option may help reduce the money you need at closing, but it creates a balance that has to be repaid later. That does not make it a bad choice. It just means you need to understand the tradeoff before you choose it.

The biggest number is not always the best path. Sometimes the right answer is the cleanest structure. Sometimes it is the larger upfront assistance. The right choice depends on your timeline, your cash position, and your long-term plans.

A Note on CHFA Assistance Amounts

A Note on CHFA Assistance Amounts

CHFA numbers can change over time, and older articles online may reference outdated assistance percentages. For 2026 planning, the key numbers to understand are:

  • Grant path: up to the lesser of $25,000 or 3% of the first mortgage
  • Second mortgage path: up to the lesser of $25,000 or 4% of the first mortgage

Final terms still depend on CHFA rules, loan type, income, credit, property, first mortgage amount, lender approval, and the specific program used. Always verify current limits directly at chfainfo.com or with a CHFA Participating Lender before building a purchase plan around any specific number.

What CHFA Could Mean on a $500,000 Colorado Home

Let's make this real.

A $500,000 home is not unusual in many Front Range communities. For a first-time buyer, that price can make the cash-to-close number feel intimidating fast.

Here is a simple illustration of what CHFA assistance could look like on a $500,000 purchase scenario:

CHFA Assistance Illustration · $500,000 Purchase
3% Grant Path Up to $25,000 or 3% of first mortgage — no repayment required
~$15,000
4% Second Mortgage Path Up to $25,000 or 4% of first mortgage — repaid at sale, refi, or payoff
~$20,000

These are illustrations only, not guaranteed amounts. Actual assistance depends on the first mortgage amount, CHFA program limits, loan structure, eligibility, lender approval, and current program rules. Verify with a CHFA Participating Lender before building a plan around any specific number.

A possible $15,000 or $20,000 assistance conversation can change how a buyer looks at the path forward.

It may not remove every cost. But it may help reduce the amount of personal savings needed at the closing table. And for many Colorado buyers, that is the difference between "not yet" and "let's run the numbers."

Who May Be a Fit for CHFA?

CHFA may be worth exploring if you are a Colorado buyer who has income and job stability but needs help with upfront costs. You may be a fit to explore CHFA if:

  • You plan to buy a home in Colorado
  • You want to reduce down payment and/or closing cost pressure
  • You can work with a CHFA Participating Lender
  • Your income fits the program limits for your county and household
  • Your purchase price fits the applicable limits
  • Your credit profile fits CHFA and lender guidelines
  • You are willing to complete CHFA-approved homebuyer education
  • You want to compare grant and second mortgage options before choosing a path

Many CHFA program matrices reference a minimum decision credit score of 620 or higher for scored borrowers. But credit is only one part of the picture. A lender will also look at your income, debt, loan type, property, assets, and full borrower profile.

So do not assume you are in or out based on one number. Use the program as a conversation starter.

If you want to compare CHFA against other Colorado assistance options, a good place to start is the Colorado home buying programs guide for essential workers.

CHFA Program Paths to Know

CHFA has several loan and assistance paths. You do not need to memorize every program name before asking questions. But a few are worth knowing about.

CHFA FirstStep

CHFA FirstStep is one CHFA mortgage path that may help eligible buyers access affordable home financing. For many buyers, this type of program is part of the broader CHFA conversation around loan options, interest rates, and assistance availability.

CHFA FirstGeneration

CHFA FirstGeneration is designed for eligible first-generation homebuyers — buyers who have not owned a home and whose parents or guardians were not homeowners during the buyer's lifetime. CHFA also notes that the program can serve certain buyers raised in foster care who have not previously owned a home.

CHFA FirstGeneration may offer up to $25,000 in down payment assistance for eligible borrowers. This can be especially meaningful for buyers trying to become the first in their family to build homeownership stability.

CHFA HomeAccess

CHFA HomeAccess is designed for certain buyers connected to disability-related eligibility. This may include eligible first-time buyers or qualified veterans with a permanent disability, or buyers who are the parent or guardian of a dependent with a permanent disability. It is a more specific path, but for the right buyer, it may be worth asking about.

You can explore all current CHFA homeownership programs at chfainfo.com/homeownership.

Can CHFA Be Combined With Other Help?

Sometimes. But this is where buyers need to be careful.

Not every assistance program can be combined with every other assistance program. Some programs are alternatives to each other. Some can work with certain loan types but not others. Some depend on the lender, property, county, and timing.

For example, CHFA and MetroDPA should not be treated as automatic stack partners. A better way to think about this is:

CHFA may be one assistance path. MetroDPA may be another. Seller credits may be another tool. VA loan benefits may be another strategy. The job is not to pile every program into one giant combination. The job is to build the right structure for your actual numbers.

A CHFA strategy may be combined with allowable seller credits in some situations. A veteran or military buyer may also want to compare how CHFA fits beside VA loan options. Other buyers may need to compare CHFA against MetroDPA, Chenoa Fund, local assistance, or other Colorado programs.

This is where a good lender and a clear strategy matter. Hero HomeReach can help you understand the questions before you start the lender conversation. You can also find answers to common down payment assistance questions at the Hero HomeReach DPA FAQ.

Questions to Ask Before Choosing CHFA

Before you choose a CHFA path, make sure you are asking the right questions. Here are the ones that matter most:

  1. Is this assistance a grant or a second mortgage?
  2. If it is a second mortgage, when would repayment be required?
  3. How much assistance could be available based on my actual first mortgage amount?
  4. What interest rate would I receive with this CHFA option?
  5. How does that rate compare with a non-CHFA loan?
  6. What income limits apply in the county where I want to buy?
  7. What purchase price limits apply?
  8. What cash will I still need for earnest money, inspections, appraisal, reserves, and closing?
  9. Can seller credits be used with this structure?
  10. Does this work with my loan type?
  11. What homebuyer education do I need to complete?
  12. What happens if I refinance or sell sooner than expected?

Those questions can save you from choosing a program based only on the assistance amount. The biggest number is not always the best path.

How to Start With CHFA

Here is a simple way to begin.

1. Understand your cash-to-close gap

How much money do you have available for the purchase? How much do you want to keep in savings after closing? How much help would make buying feel realistic? You do not need perfect answers yet. You just need a starting point.

2. Compare the grant and second mortgage options

The CHFA grant path may be attractive because it does not require repayment. The second mortgage path may be attractive because it can provide more assistance. Neither is automatically better. The right one depends on your timeline, cash position, loan type, and long-term plans.

3. Check income and purchase price limits

CHFA income limits vary by program and household profile. Do not guess. Check the current limits or ask a CHFA Participating Lender to review your situation.

4. Plan for homebuyer education

CHFA homebuyer education is part of the process. Instead of seeing it as a hurdle, treat it as a way to understand the terms before you sign anything. A little clarity upfront can protect you from expensive confusion later.

5. Talk with a CHFA Participating Lender

Because CHFA does not lend directly to consumers, you will need to work through a CHFA Participating Lender. That lender can help determine which CHFA loan program and down payment assistance option may fit.

6. Map CHFA against your other options

CHFA may be a strong path. But it may not be the only path. Depending on your situation, you may also want to compare MetroDPA, Chenoa Fund, VA loan strategies, FHA or conventional options, seller credits, local assistance programs, and employer or community-based help.

Is CHFA Only for First-Time Homebuyers?

Not always.

Some CHFA programs are built for first-time buyers or specific buyer groups. Others may be available to repeat buyers, depending on the program and loan structure. This is one reason CHFA can feel confusing — "CHFA loan" does not always mean one single eligibility box.

Your best next step is to check the specific CHFA program that matches your situation, then verify it with a CHFA Participating Lender.

Is CHFA Better Than MetroDPA?

Not automatically.

CHFA is statewide and offers grant and second mortgage assistance structures. MetroDPA serves eligible areas tied to participating Front Range communities and is known for assistance that may be forgivable after a required occupancy period.

One is not universally better. The better path depends on where you are buying, your income, your loan type, your timeline, your credit profile, your cash-to-close gap, and which lenders and programs are available for your situation.

CHFA may be a strong fit for one buyer. MetroDPA may be a better fit for another. The smartest move is to compare both before falling in love with one headline number.

The Bottom Line on CHFA Loans in Colorado

CHFA can be one of the most important homebuyer assistance paths to understand in Colorado.

For eligible buyers, it may help reduce the upfront cash needed through either a grant or a deferred second mortgage.

The grant path may offer less assistance but no repayment requirement. The second mortgage path may offer more assistance but must be repaid later when certain events happen. Both can matter depending on your situation.

If you are a Colorado teacher, firefighter, EMT, police officer, nurse, healthcare worker, veteran, active-duty service member, military spouse, or public service professional, CHFA is worth exploring as part of your broader homebuying plan.

Not because it guarantees anything. Because it may change the question from "How could I ever save enough?" to "What assistance path actually fits my numbers?"

That is a much better place to start.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a CHFA loan in Colorado?

A CHFA loan is a Colorado home loan program offered through the Colorado Housing and Finance Authority's network of participating lenders. CHFA loan programs may be paired with down payment and/or closing cost assistance for eligible buyers.

Does CHFA offer down payment assistance?

Yes. CHFA offers down payment and closing cost assistance through a grant option and a second mortgage option. Each structure has different benefits, limits, and repayment rules. You can review current program details at chfainfo.com/homeownership/down-payment-assistance.

Is CHFA assistance a grant or a loan?

CHFA offers both. The grant option is described as assistance with no repayment required. The second mortgage option offers deferred repayment and becomes due when certain events happen, such as sale, refinance, payoff of the first mortgage, or the home no longer being your primary residence.

Do I have to repay CHFA down payment assistance?

It depends on the assistance type. The CHFA grant option does not require repayment. The CHFA second mortgage option does require repayment later when certain triggering events occur.

Do I need to be a first-time homebuyer for CHFA?

Not always. Some CHFA programs are designed for first-time buyers or specific buyer groups, while other CHFA paths may be available to repeat buyers. The specific program matters.

Can veterans use CHFA?

Yes, veterans may be able to use CHFA depending on the program, loan type, lender approval, and full borrower profile. Some CHFA paths also include qualified veteran eligibility features.

What credit score do I need for a CHFA loan?

Many CHFA program matrices reference a minimum decision credit score of 620 or higher for scored borrowers. Final approval depends on the full loan file, including income, debt, assets, loan type, property, and lender review.

Can CHFA be used with seller credits?

In some cases, seller credits may be part of the buyer's overall closing cost strategy. Whether seller credits work with your CHFA structure depends on loan type, program rules, lender approval, and the details of the transaction.

Is CHFA better than MetroDPA?

Neither is automatically better. CHFA and MetroDPA are different assistance paths with different geographic coverage, structures, repayment rules, and eligibility guidelines. The better option depends on your specific numbers and where you are buying.

How do I apply for a CHFA loan?

You apply through a CHFA Participating Lender. CHFA does not lend directly to consumers. A participating lender can help you review program options, income limits, loan type, assistance structure, and next steps.
