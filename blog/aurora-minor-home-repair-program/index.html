Aurora Minor Home Repair Program: Up to $15,000

Aurora homeowners may soon have another chance to access up to $15,000 for eligible repairs and accessibility improvements. The City currently asks residents to check back in November 2026, which means now is the time to prepare, not wait, before applications begin moving again.

Hero HomeReach is tracking public Aurora updates so you do not have to rely on old program pages or scattered information.

Quick Answer

What may be available: Aurora's Minor Home Repair Program may help income eligible homeowners with accessibility, electrical, plumbing, roofing, and HVAC repairs. Brothers Redevelopment describes assistance of up to $15,000, based on public information.

Who it may be for: Aurora homeowners who own and occupy their home as a primary residence and meet current income limits.

Current status: The City currently pauses new applications and asks residents to check back in November 2026, a date that is not guaranteed.

Why preparation matters: Participation is limited to one time per applicant, and the application requires significant documentation. Homeowners who prepare now may move faster once applications return.

Why This Opportunity Matters

Up to $15,000 in repair assistance could materially change what an Aurora homeowner can afford to fix. A roll-in shower conversion or a wheelchair ramp. A panel upgrade so a home's electrical system passes inspection. A failing water heater or a furnace that will not survive another Colorado winter. A roof nearing the end of its life. For a homeowner facing one or more of these repairs, this is exactly the kind of opportunity worth preparing for now.

None of this is promised to any individual applicant, and the City's own page does not confirm $15,000 as an official cap. But the potential is real enough, and the preparation work is involved enough, that waiting until reopening day to start is the biggest mistake a homeowner can make.

Why Preparing Now Creates an Advantage

The application is not a quick sign up. Aurora's process may ask for mortgage statements, insurance records, tax returns, months of bank statements, identification, and proof of income for every adult in the household. Gathering all of that after applications reopen puts a homeowner behind everyone who started early.

Participation is also limited to one time per applicant, which means the scope of a first application matters. A homeowner who has already thought through their full repair need, not just the most urgent item, is in a stronger position than one figuring it out in real time.

Treat the months before reopening as a head start, not paperwork to dread. Everything gathered now is one less thing standing between a homeowner and a completed application later.

The Two $15,000 Numbers

Here is a detail worth understanding closely, because it is easy to misread.

Brothers Redevelopment describes the Minor Home Repair Program as providing up to $15,000 in home rehabilitation services. That is the potential repair benefit.

Separately, the official application packet lists a different $15,000 figure: a limit on combined bank and savings reserves for everyone on the property title. Some applicants who are 62 or older, living with a qualifying disability, or on fixed income may be permitted up to $30,000 in reserves, subject to current rules.

One $15,000 may describe what a homeowner could receive. The other $15,000 may describe what a homeowner is allowed to have saved. Confusing the two could mean assuming you qualify when a reserve limit actually applies, or assuming you do not qualify when only the benefit amount needs confirming.

Before applying, ask Aurora directly: does the current $15,000 figure describe the repair benefit, the reserve limit, or both? Get the answer in writing when possible.

What the Program May Cover

Based on the City's public description, Minor Home Repair may cover:

  • Accessibility: grab bars, roll-in showers, wheelchair ramps
  • Electrical: panel upgrades, wiring repairs
  • Plumbing: pipe repairs, fixture replacement
  • Roofing: repair or replacement tied to normal wear, not disaster damage
  • HVAC: furnace and related system repairs

The exact project scope still depends on inspection, current funding, and program approval. Do not sign a repair contract or begin work before written approval. Work completed before approval may not be covered.

Who May Be Eligible

  • Owns and occupies the property as a primary residence
  • Home is within Aurora city limits
  • Meets current HUD household income limits
  • Is current on all debt secured against the property
  • Has no liens or judgments
  • Carries a current homeowners insurance policy
  • Has 12 months of current mortgage payment history
  • Has not previously participated in this program (one time per applicant)

What to Gather Before Applications Return

Use this as a working checklist while the program is paused:

  • Current mortgage statement
  • Homeowners insurance declarations page
  • Most recent federal tax return, plus W2 and 1099 forms
  • Three months of complete bank and savings statements for every adult in the household
  • Current benefit statements, if applicable
  • Colorado identification for every adult
  • A defined list of the repairs you would request, in priority order
  • Questions about the current reserve limit, based on your own savings

Remember two rules while you prepare: participation is one time only, and work should never begin before written approval.

Aurora Minor Home Repair Program: What to Know Before You Apply

Program Guide July 2026 8 min read
Share

Aurora homeowners may have heard there is help for accessibility work, plumbing, electrical repairs, roofing, or HVAC problems. But before you count on that help, there are three details that could completely change your plan.

The program is currently paused. The widely repeated $15,000 figure has two different meanings. And the City's public materials do not clearly say whether Minor Home Repair assistance is a grant or a repayable obligation.

That does not mean the program is not worth exploring. It means you need a clearer map before you gather documents, delay an urgent repair, or assume help will be available.

Quick Answer

Aurora's Minor Home Repair Program may help income eligible owner occupants with accessibility, electrical, plumbing, roofing, and HVAC work. The program is temporarily paused, and the City directs homeowners to return in November 2026. The application also includes income, mortgage history, insurance, lien, and reserve requirements. Confirm the current repair cap and repayment structure directly with Aurora before relying on either.

Surprise One: You Cannot Apply Right Now

The City of Aurora currently says the Minor Home Repair Program is temporarily pausing applications and directs residents to return in November 2026.

That status matters because older Brothers Redevelopment pages still describe the program without mentioning the pause.

A homeowner could read an older page, begin collecting documents, and believe an application is moving forward today. The City's live page controls the current status.

Before doing anything else, call Aurora Housing and Community Development at 303 739 7900 or check the official Aurora housing rehabilitation programs page.

Program pauses can change. Treat November as the City's current direction, not a guaranteed reopening date.

Surprise Two: There Are Two Different $15,000 Numbers

This is the detail most likely to trip up a careful homeowner.

Brothers Redevelopment describes the Aurora Minor Home Repair Program as providing up to $15,000 in home rehabilitation services.

But the current City program summary does not publish a repair benefit cap.

The official application packet uses another $15,000 figure for something completely different. It generally limits combined bank and savings reserves for people on title to $15,000.

Certain applicants who are age 62 or older, live with a qualifying disability, or receive fixed income may be permitted up to $30,000 in combined savings.

So there may be two separate questions:

  1. How much repair work may the program cover?
  2. How much money may the household hold in bank and savings accounts?

Those numbers should never be treated as interchangeable.

A homeowner with $16,000 saved for medical care, tuition, or emergencies could hear "up to $15,000" and assume it describes only the repair benefit. The reserve test may become the more important number in their application.

Before applying, ask Aurora:

Does the current $15,000 figure describe the repair benefit, the reserve limit, or both?

Get the answer in writing when possible.

Surprise Three: Repair Assistance Does Not Automatically Mean Grant

Aurora operates three separate housing rehabilitation programs using federal Community Development Block Grant funding.

The City clearly describes:

  • Essential Home Repair as a grant
  • Home Repair Loan as a zero percent deferred loan
  • Minor Home Repair without clearly stating whether it is a grant or loan

That silence matters.

Federal CDBG money can be delivered through grants, loans, or other financing structures. The federal funding source alone does not tell you whether repayment or a lien may apply.

Until Aurora confirms the current Minor Home Repair structure, avoid assuming:

  • The work never has to be repaid
  • No lien will be recorded
  • The assistance is automatically a grant
  • Selling or refinancing will have no effect

Ask this directly:

Is the Minor Home Repair Program a grant, or could it create a deferred repayment obligation or lien on my home?

That one question could prevent a painful surprise years later.

What the Program May Cover

The City lists repairs related to:

  • Accessibility
  • Electrical systems
  • Plumbing
  • Roofing
  • Heating, ventilation, and air conditioning

Brothers Redevelopment also describes accessibility modifications, deferred maintenance, and general rehabilitation services for income qualified Aurora homeowners.

The exact project scope still depends on inspection, current funding, and program approval.

Do not sign a contract or begin work because a repair sounds eligible. Work completed before written approval may not be covered.

What the Program Does Not Cover

Aurora says its housing rehabilitation programs cannot assist with disaster relief items such as:

  • Leaking roofs tied to disaster damage
  • Structural flooding
  • Standing water
  • Broken or leaking windows caused by a disaster
  • Sewer line backups
  • Basement repairs related to disaster conditions

Homeowners with disaster related damage are directed to FEMA.

This distinction is easy to miss. A roof may be a covered repair in one situation and excluded in another because the cause was storm or disaster damage.

If the repair is urgent, ask the City which program or agency fits before waiting for Minor Home Repair to reopen.

Who May Be Eligible

The City's current published requirements say an applicant must:

  • Own the property
  • Live in the property as a primary residence
  • Have a home inside Aurora city limits
  • Meet current HUD household income limits
  • Be current on all debt secured by the property
  • Have no liens or judgments
  • Carry current homeowners insurance
  • Show 12 months of current mortgage payment history
  • Have a property that needs eligible home repairs

Participation is limited to one time per applicant.

That one time rule deserves attention. Do not assume you can submit a small request now and return next year for a second repair.

Ask the program specialist whether all related eligible work should be evaluated in one application.

Aurora uses HUD income limits for its housing rehabilitation programs. The City lists the following maximum gross annual income figures, effective June 1, 2026:

Household income is broader than one person's salary.

The application asks about wages, overtime, bonuses, commissions, Social Security, disability benefits, pensions, unemployment, worker's compensation, alimony, child support, and other income for adults in the household.

Use the live City table before applying. Older online articles may still show previous years' limits.

The Application Is More Serious Than It Looks

This is not a five minute city sign up.

The application packet may require:

  • Homeowners insurance declarations
  • Current mortgage statements
  • Information about every lien on the property
  • Three months of complete bank and savings statements
  • The most recent federal tax return
  • W2 and 1099 forms
  • Current benefit statements
  • Three months of pay stubs
  • Colorado identification
  • Divorce or support documents when applicable
  • Signed affidavits and authorizations
  • Social Security Numbers for the applicant and co applicant
  • Lead safety acknowledgments for homes built before 1978

The City is not only checking whether the repair looks necessary. It is verifying ownership, occupancy, income, reserves, insurance, mortgage history, and property debt.

A missing bank statement page or unexplained account can slow the process.

A Homeowner Could Do Everything Right and Still Be Surprised

Imagine an Aurora homeowner who needs a safer shower after surgery.

They find an older article saying the program may provide up to $15,000. They assume it is open. They assume the work is a grant. Then they learn:

  • Applications are paused
  • Three months of bank statements are required
  • Household savings may affect eligibility
  • The repair amount needs current confirmation
  • The repayment structure is not clearly stated online

That homeowner did not fail to research. The information was scattered.

Hero HomeReach exists for that gap.

What to Do Before November

First, confirm the program status

Call the City before relying on the reopening date.

Second, ask the two questions nobody should skip

  1. What is the current maximum repair benefit?
  2. Is the assistance a grant, or can it create a lien or repayment obligation?

Third, check income and reserves

Compare your household's gross income with the current City table.

Review bank and savings balances for everyone whose finances may be counted. If you are near the $15,000 or $30,000 reserve threshold, ask what accounts count before assuming the answer.

Fourth, start your document folder

Gather:

  • Tax return
  • Mortgage statement
  • Insurance declarations
  • Three months of bank statements
  • Pay stubs
  • Benefit letters
  • IDs

Fifth, define the full repair need

Because participation is one time only, ask whether related accessibility and safety work should be evaluated together.

Buying First, Repairing Later

The Minor Home Repair Program is not purchase assistance.

If you are still planning to buy, start with our guide to financing and modifying an accessible home in Aurora. It explains why purchase financing and later repair assistance must be planned on separate timelines.

Households connected to a permanent disability may also want to review CHFA HomeAccess and HomeAccess Plus. Those programs apply to the purchase side, not the repair application described here.

Questions to Ask Aurora Before You Apply

Take this list into your first call:

  • Is Minor Home Repair accepting applications today?
  • What is the current maximum repair benefit?
  • Is the assistance a grant or a repayable obligation?
  • Could a lien be recorded against the home?
  • What counts toward the reserve limit?
  • Are retirement or earmarked accounts included?
  • Does my repair fall under Minor, Essential, or the Home Repair Loan Program?
  • Can accessibility and safety repairs be reviewed together?
  • What happens if my income changes while I wait?
  • How long does review and inspection usually take?
