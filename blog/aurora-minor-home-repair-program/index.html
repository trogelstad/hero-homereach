Aurora Minor Home Repair Program: Up to $15,000

Aurora homeowners may soon have another chance to access up to $15,000 for eligible repairs and accessibility improvements. The City currently asks residents to check back in November 2026, which means now is the time to prepare, not wait, before applications begin moving again.

Hero HomeReach is tracking public Aurora updates so you do not have to rely on old program pages or scattered information.

Quick Answer

What may be available: Aurora's Minor Home Repair Program may help income eligible homeowners with accessibility, electrical, plumbing, roofing, and HVAC repairs. Brothers Redevelopment describes assistance of up to $15,000, based on public information.

Who it may be for: Aurora homeowners who own and occupy their home as a primary residence and meet current income limits.

Current status: The City currently pauses new applications and asks residents to check back in November 2026, a date that is not guaranteed.

Why preparation matters: Participation is limited to one time per applicant, and the application requires significant documentation. Homeowners who prepare now may move faster once applications return.

The Reality Check: The Pause and the 12-Month Rule

Up to $15,000 in repair assistance could materially change what an Aurora homeowner can afford to fix. A roll-in shower conversion or a wheelchair ramp. A panel upgrade so a home's electrical system passes inspection. A failing water heater or a furnace that will not survive another Colorado winter. A roof nearing the end of its life. For a homeowner facing one or more of these repairs, this is exactly the kind of opportunity worth preparing for now.

Here is the honest part. The City of Aurora currently pauses new Minor Home Repair applications, and the projected return is November 2026. That date is not guaranteed. It is the timeline the City currently asks residents to plan around, and it could shift.

There is a second rule that matters just as much as the pause itself, and almost nobody explains it clearly: the 12-month mortgage history requirement. To qualify, Aurora requires 12 months of current, on-time mortgage payment history on the property. Not six months. Not "mostly on time." A full year, current the whole way through.

Think about what that means for a homeowner buying in Aurora right now, including one using CHFA HomeAccess to finance an accessible home today. Even if Minor Home Repair reopens exactly on schedule, a brand new homeowner cannot touch that repair money until they have logged a full year of payments. So a buyer closing this fall would not be eligible until roughly a year after closing regardless of when the program reopens.

That is not a reason to feel discouraged. It is the reason the current pause is actually useful. The clock on both timelines, the City's reopening window and your own 12-month mortgage history, is running whether you prepare or not. The only question is whether your documents, your repair scope, and your questions for Aurora are ready when both clocks finish counting.

The Two $15,000 Numbers

Here is a detail worth understanding closely, because it is easy to misread.

Brothers Redevelopment describes the Minor Home Repair Program as providing up to $15,000 in home rehabilitation services. That is the potential repair benefit.

Separately, the official application packet lists a different $15,000 figure: a limit on combined bank and savings reserves for everyone on the property title. Some applicants who are 62 or older, living with a qualifying disability, or on fixed income may be permitted up to $30,000 in reserves, subject to current rules.

One $15,000 may describe what a homeowner could receive. The other $15,000 may describe what a homeowner is allowed to have saved. Confusing the two could mean assuming you qualify when a reserve limit actually applies, or assuming you do not qualify when only the benefit amount needs confirming.

Before applying, ask Aurora directly: does the current $15,000 figure describe the repair benefit, the reserve limit, or both? Get the answer in writing when possible.

The CHFA HomeAccess "Income Shield"

Here is something worth understanding before you even buy the home, especially for multi-generational households.

Municipal repair programs like Minor Home Repair generally count income the way Aurora's own application does: gross income for every adult 18 or older living in the household, not just the homeowner. That definition can quietly work against exactly the households these programs are meant to help. A disabled homeowner who lives with an adult child who helps with caregiving, or a live-in aide, or a grown grandchild paying rent to help out, can find that person's income counted toward the household total, even though it is not the homeowner's own money and was never meant to fund home repairs.

Buying the home with CHFA HomeAccess works differently, and the difference matters. Based on CHFA's published income limits guidance, HomeAccess qualifying income counts only the borrower and co-borrower on the loan, plus any spouse or civil union partner residing in the home, plus anyone who is expected to live in the home and be secondarily liable on the mortgage. An adult child, a live-in aide, or another occupant who is not on the mortgage and not secondarily liable is not counted toward CHFA's $178,920 statewide qualifying income limit.

In plain English: the mortgage you choose today can shield the rest of your household's income from counting against you tomorrow, at least for CHFA's own limit. This is exactly the kind of detail worth discussing with a CHFA-participating lender before you buy, not after. It does not change how Aurora's Minor Home Repair program will count income for its own limits, and that separate question still needs its own confirmation. The Federal Red Tape: Why You Can't Just Hire a Handyman

One more thing worth knowing before you get your hopes up about a quick fix: this is federal money, and federal money comes with strings attached.

Minor Home Repair is funded through a federal Community Development Block Grant, administered by the City of Aurora with Brothers Redevelopment handling delivery. CDBG funding generally requires the administering organization to manage contractor selection, licensing verification, and compliance steps like lead-safe work practices for homes built before 1978, rather than simply handing a homeowner a check to spend with any contractor of their choosing.

Based on Brothers Redevelopment's own published information, its Home Modification and Repair program uses vetted, background-checked staff and a network of subcontractors it selects through its own proposal process, not a contractor a homeowner brings in independently. That is a reasonable, common way CDBG-funded repair programs operate, and it is generally a good thing for quality control, but it also means you should not assume you can simply hire your nephew's contractor friend at a discount and get reimbursed.

Ask Aurora and Brothers Redevelopment directly: will I be able to choose my own licensed contractor, or does the work have to go through Brothers Redevelopment's own crew or approved subcontractor network? Get the answer before you plan around a specific contractor or price.

What the Program May Cover

Based on the City's public description, Minor Home Repair may cover:

  • Accessibility: grab bars, roll-in showers, wheelchair ramps
  • Electrical: panel upgrades, wiring repairs
  • Plumbing: pipe repairs, fixture replacement
  • Roofing: repair or replacement tied to normal wear, not disaster damage
  • HVAC: furnace and related system repairs

The exact project scope still depends on inspection, current funding, and program approval. Do not sign a repair contract or begin work before written approval. Work completed before approval may not be covered.

Who May Be Eligible

  • Owns and occupies the property as a primary residence
  • Home is within Aurora city limits
  • Meets current HUD household income limits
  • Is current on all debt secured against the property
  • Has no liens or judgments
  • Carries a current homeowners insurance policy
  • Has 12 months of current mortgage payment history
  • Has not previously participated in this program (one time per applicant)

What to Gather Before Applications Return

Use this as a working checklist while the program is paused:

  • Current mortgage statement
  • Homeowners insurance declarations page
  • Most recent federal tax return, plus W2 and 1099 forms
  • Three months of complete bank and savings statements for every adult in the household
  • Current benefit statements, if applicable
  • Colorado identification for every adult
  • A defined list of the repairs you would request, in priority order
  • Questions about the current reserve limit, based on your own savings

Remember two rules while you prepare: participation is one time only, and work should never begin before written approval.

Which Aurora Repair Program Fits

Minor Home Repair is one of three City of Aurora housing rehabilitation programs. Here is how they currently compare:

Grant, per the City's descriptionStructure not clearly stated by the CityZero percent deferred loan, per the City's description
Urgent, safety related system failures (furnace, water heater, sewer or water main)Accessibility, electrical, plumbing, roofing, HVACLarger scope repairs: roofing, windows, furnaces, insulation, electrical, plumbing
